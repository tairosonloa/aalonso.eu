Why I chose ArchLinux as my main GNU/Linux distro

Versión en español
February 11, 2018

Some people have asked me why I chose ArchLinux as my main GNU/Linux distro, and why not Ubuntu or its parent Debian. Well, I'll explain why I'm in love with my ArchLinux installation.

I think there are three many reasons for what I tested ArchLinux and I still stay here, more than two years later. Those are its documentation (and its community), with its huge and fully wiki; its philosophy (hackable and rolling release), and its packages manager, pacman. I really love its packages manager.

Wiki and community

I started in the GNU/Linux world with Debian, and it was many the times that I had a problem (so usual in a newbie user like I was) because I didn't understand something, or I wanted to do something and I didn't know how... or touching my installation I broked something and I had to fix it (so usual too at those times). So when I searched on the internet for help, always the ArchLinux wiki came to the rescue. As I said, it's huge and fully documented, and it was (and it is) so useful to understand the main of GNU/Linux. There is all well explained, sometimes with examples, and nowadays it still helps me when I need it.

Its community is also very good compared to other distributions. People are patient with newbie users, and always trying to help. The community is very active, especially in the forums. There is also a users repository (AUR) of which I'll tell you later. Going back to the wiki, a good way to start contributing to free software is helping on its documentation. I help to complete and update the wiki like many users do. I also help to translate pages from English to Spanish. I invite you to do the same with your favorite free software project.

ArchLinux philosophy

About its philosophy, it adjusts perfectly to my way of doing the things. I'm a type of user that likes to have its system clean and to know everything that happens on it and everything that is installed on it. Also, I love to configure my system and learn in the process about how the things work. The ArchLinux install process push you on that way because the installation is entirely by terminal commands, and after that, the system you get has the minimum and essentially software possible. It's such a clean installation that even it hasn't X support or a desktop environment by default. If you want X, you must install it. This is a perfect way to make your own system, to know what it has and what it has not, and to learn in the process.

Also, I'm a kind of a geek that loves on having and trying the most recent version of a software, without needing to wait for months for my distro maintainers to release that version on the official repositories. I hate the frozen distros like Debian or Ubuntu because of the same reason (of many others), and also because a distro upgrade can break everything, what it's "impossible" on ArchLinux, because it's rolling release and it will be always up to date.

To finish writing about ArchLinux philosophy, I want to highlight that the official ArchLinux position is KISS (Keep It Simple Stupid), and I think they really achieve it with the type of system you get after a fresh install, and the marvelous work of its packages manager. Indeed the installation process can be scary, but it's also full documented on the wiki, the steps are very simple and you learn a lot in the process.

Pacman, more than a packages manager

Well, I'm really in love with that packages manager. Pacman is really a good piece of software that makes the process to install, uninstall, purge and manage your packages easy peasy. There were some things of ArchLinux that attracted me to install it like I have just explained in the previous paragraphs, but the first thing that did me feel at home and to know I wasn't wrong was pacman. It manages packages dependencies with skill and in all this time I never have had a problem with broken packages, dependencies or similar (very usual in my times using apt or apt-get in Debian and Ubuntu).

Also, when you deinstall a package, you can purge all its dependencies that aren't used from another package and also delete its config files and everything you can imagine with a single command. For sure, pacman is still more powerful, but I cannot dedicate all the article to speak about its wonders. You can check all its functions on the wiki.

And there is the users repository. More specifically, the ArchLinux Users Repositories (AUR). What does that mean? It means that it doesn't matter what package or software are you looking for. If you can't find it on the distro official repositories, you can be sure it will be on the AUR. Don't you believe me? Try it! Users upload to AUR a PKGBUILD (a script to automate the installation of a external package) that you can download. It serves to get the packages sources, its dependencies, compile it (if needed) and install it using pacman. Also, there are AUR helpers that simplify it even more. In my case I use pacaur.

These are my reasons

So that's it. I'm in love with ArchLinux, and you can see I have reasons to. If I have convinced you and you want to install it, you can donwload ArchLinux from here and check the installation guide here. Welcome to this marvelous community!
